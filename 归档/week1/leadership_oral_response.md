# Leadership Discussion: Oral Response (General Version)

"In our group discussion, we identified several core qualities that differentiate good leaders from bad ones based on organizational behavior theories.

Starting with the positives, a good leader must possess high **Emotional Intelligence** to empathize with their team and maintain stability during high-pressure situations. **Integrity and Honesty** are equally vital; when a leader admits their own mistakes and follows a clear ethical code, they build lasting **Trust** within the organization. We also believe being **Visionary and Transformational** is essential, as it inspires employees to look beyond their personal interests and work toward a shared, meaningful future. Furthermore, a **Consideration** or people-oriented approach—showing genuine care for employees' personal needs and career growth—is key to sustaining high job satisfaction. Finally, **Confidence and Decisiveness** allow a leader to make tough, timely calls when the market is uncertain or resources are limited.

On the other hand, we discussed how certain negative traits can ruin a team's culture. **Narcissism** is a major flaw, as it often leads a leader to prioritize their own ego and public image over collective success. **Micromanagement** is another frequent issue, as it destroys employee **Autonomy** and hampers creative problem-solving. We also highlighted that a **Lack of Accountability**—shifting blame to subordinates or external factors when targets are missed—completely erodes a leader's credibility. **Poor Communication**, where instructions are vague or feedback is never given, leads to massive confusion and workplace stress. Lastly, **Volatility** and emotional instability create a fear-based environment where staff are afraid to speak up or innovate for fear of a negative reaction.

In conclusion, effective leadership isn't just about functional skills; it's about balancing these psychological and behavioral traits to foster a healthy, productive organization."
